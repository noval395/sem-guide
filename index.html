Course Overview

Understand the course and its general contents

What is AP Seminar?

AP Seminar allows you to explore real world topics/issues of your choice. In this class you will develop the ability to properly analyze and evaluate information from various perspectives, use that analysis to develop arguments, and communicate your ideas in written essays and presentations.

Unlike many other courses that focus on a specific skills or topic, what you learn in this class will be transferable across many classes and into your future. You will improve your critical thinking ability, reasoning, use of evidence, argumentative writing ability, effective communication, slideshow design, and the understanding of multiple perspectives.

The Five Big Ideas

Throughout AP Seminar you will focus on Five Big Ideas outlined by College Board, they are the following:

1

Question and Explore

Learn to identify problems, develop questions, find information, and evaluate sources from multiple perspectives.

2

Understand and Analyze

Read critically, explain arguments, analyze reasoning, and evaluate evidence used by authors.

3

Evaluate Multiple Perspectives

Learn to compare different viewpoints, evaluate implications of something, and understand issues.

4

Synthesize Ideas

Form conclusions, build arguments using various sources, and develop effective solutions based on evidence.

5

Team, Transform, and Transmit

Learn working in groups, work collaboratively, plan presentations, and communicate effectively.

AP Capstone: AP Seminar is the first course in the AP Capstone Diploma Program. Taking both AP Seminar and AP Research can earn you an AP Capstone Diploma, which may stand out to colleges.

Assessments

Understanding the three components that make up your AP score

Unlike normal AP's where your score is based on a final exam, in Seminar a majority of your score is based on work completed throughout the year and an exam. In total, your final AP Score is based on three components:

Team Project and Presentation (TMP)

20% of Final Score

What it is: Work in teams of 3-5 to identify an area for investigation, research the issue, and create a presentation defending your identified position/solution.

Components:

  • Individual Research Report (1,200 words)
  • Team Presentation
  • Oral Defense (1 Question Per Member)

Scoring: Individual Research Report is scored by College Board, Presentation scored by teacher.

Individual Written Argument & Presentation (IWA + IMP)

35% of Final Score

What it is: Using stimulus material provided by College Board and identify an overarching theme or topic to further research, develop your own area for research based on the stimulus, write a paper and presentation to defend your findings/position/solution.

Components:

  • Written Essay (2,000 Words)
  • Oral Presentation (6-8 Minutes)

Scoring: Individual Written Argument is scored by College Board, Presentation scored by teacher.

End-of-Course Exam (EOC)

45% of Final Score

What it is: 2 hour closed digital exam testing the analytical and writing skills you've developed through the year.

Components:

  • 3 Short Answer Questions (Based on 1 Provided Source)
  • 1 Essay Response (Using 4 Provided Sources)

Scoring: Scored by College Board readers.

End-of-Course Exam

A breakdown of the final exam and tips for success

Exam Format & Structure

The End of Course Exam is a 2 hour closed test with no breaks. It will consist of two parts testing the analytical and writing skills you've practiced throughout the year, think of it as applying what you've learned from the IRR and IWA. The exam will contribute to 45% of your final score.

Exam Breakdown

Part A: Short-Answer Questions

30% of EOC Score (13.5% of Final AP Score)

Description: You will be presented a single source, you are to identify the authors argument, line of reasoning, and the credibility/effectiveness of their evidence.

Skills Tested:

  • Explain and analyze an argument
  • Identify line of reasoning
  • Evaluate evidence and claims

Time Suggestion: ~30 minutes total

Part B: Essay Question

70% of EOC Score (31.5% of Final AP Score)

Description: You will be given 4 sources all with a common overarching theme, are you to identify this theme and construct your own argument using at least two of the given sources as evidence.

Skills Tested:

  • Synthesize information from multiple sources
  • Create an evidence-based argument
  • Evaluate multiple perspectives

Time Suggestion: ~90 minutes

Timing Suggestions: The listed 30 and 90 minutes are suggested estimates, take time as you see necessary just keep in mind Part B will likely be more time consuming.

Common Mistakes & Tips

📝 Part A Tips

  • Consider credibility when evaluating evidence.
  • Consider bias when evaluating evidence.
  • Try to understand the authors argument at the beginning.
  • When evaluating effectiveness of evidence introduce the claim, the evidence it is supported by, state weather it is effective or not, then elaborate on why.

✍️ Part B Tips

  • Create your own unique argument, don't just repeat or reword sources.
  • Complete one read through of all 4 sources before jumping into writing.
  • Take time to plan your essay before writing, make sure you have enough time to do this.
  • Do not make your statements 100% assertive, soften them, you have limited evidence (ex: "this IS why" vs "a potential explanation for").

Question Development

Creating an effective research question

Building a Strong Research Question

Here are a few things to consider when creating your research question for the IWA and IRR:

🎯 Balanced Question

The question should be narrow enough to deeply investigate, but not too specific and still remain broad enough so that it is still researchable.

⚖️ Room for Debate

A good research question should be arguable and there should be room for debate, there should be area for people to disagree with. Don't create questions with obvious or undisputed answers.

🚫 Avoid Nested Questions

Do not create a nested question. A nested question is a question inside of another question where the second question commonly relies on an answer to the first.

🔍 Knowing the Answer

You should not have an answer to your question as soon as it's developed and before you have researched. If you do it likely isn't a good question. You are researching with the purpose of finding the answer.

❌ Avoid Yes or No Questions

The answer to your question should not be a one word "yes" or "no", for example if it's a position based question: "We should ___ because ___"

Research Process: If you have a definitive answer to your question as soon as you develop it, and before researching, it likely isn't a good question. You are researching to find that conclusion.

Solution Based vs. Position Based

🔧 Solution Based Questions

Establish, recognize, and prove the existence of an issue, and argue for a proposed solution(s) with evidence.

Examples:

  • "How should..."
  • "How can ___ be addressed..."

📍 Position Based Questions

Take a position on an issue or controversy/debate and logically support your position using evidence.

Examples:

  • "Should..."
  • "What is the most effective..."
  • "To what extent..."
